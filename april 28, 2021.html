Welcome to Wednesday, Walter readers. Today is April 28, 2021. After refusing induction into the Army on this date in 1967, Muhammed Ali was stripped of his boxing license and was later convicted of violating the Universal Military Training and Service Act. He was sentenced to five years in prison and fined $10,000 for his transgression, but after multiple appeals he was able to return to boxing in 1970 without having served time. His conviction was later overturned by the Supreme Court in 1971. Sports activism today
It’s been nearly five years since San Francisco 49ers QB Colin Kaepernick first began making waves by sitting during the national anthem in a preseason game. He did so as a protest against racial injustice, saying: “I am not going to stand up to show pride in a flag for a country that oppresses black people and people of color.” With that comment, Kaepernick didn’t exactly usher in a new era of athlete activism, but he did expand its reach and visibility.

There’s a long history of sports stars using their skills and platforms to offer social commentary. From Muhammed Ali (see above) to Bill Russell to the Black Power salutes of Tommie Smith and John Carlos, many of these protests are etched in our memory. But with the advent of social media and a more polarized media landscape in general, being asked about contentious topics is part of the job for athletes these days. 

Perhaps the most visible figure in today’s activist environment is basketball superstar Lebron James, who sparked controversy in 2012 when he tweeted a photo of the entire Miami Heat team posing in hoodies, a reference to the police killing of Florida teenager Trayvon Martin. He continues to speak out on matters of police brutality, gun violence, sexism and voting rights, and many would argue it was his voice that led the NBA to allow players to wear messages of social justice on their uniforms last July.

And while we may have become accustomed to an increasingly “woke” sports world, it shouldn’t be overlooked that Lebron James is one of the most visible and talented athletes of his generation. While Michael Jordan avoided controversy at all costs, and allegedly said, “Republicans buy sneakers, too” during his playing days, James has become perhaps the most important and influential athlete since Muhammed Ali to make activism part of his public persona.

He has been told to “shut up and dribble” and been mocked by a former President, but James’s actions speak louder than the words of many of his critics. From his I Promise School in Akron to donating time and millions of dollars to organizations like the Boys and Girls Clubs of America and the Children’s Defense Fund, James puts his money and his brand where his mouth is. In doing so, he’s helping to create a generation of athletes who will have the opportunity (and the responsibility) to take a stand, even if that sometimes means taking a knee.
